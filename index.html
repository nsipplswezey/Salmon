The Salmon Game

Exploring the Life Cycle Strategies of Salmon Using Computational Modeling

by Nick Sippl-Swezey and Bryan Flaig

The world is complicated. How do we explore it? How do we use what we know to make decisions about what to do next?

We model the world. And most times, we model it without even thinking about it. We model intuitively and reflexively.

As we proceed through life, we collect a lot of data. And we have to organize it somehow. So we build mental models that try to organize the information we've collected. We often test those models against data, and we reevaluate our models based on what we observe.

How did you know what time you had to leave the house to get to work this morning? Over your life time, you've collected a lot of data about being on time. You've likely built a pretty good model, and at some level maybe it includes how many miles you have to travel, how many minutes it takes to travel that distance, how much more time it takes if you hit traffic, and what consequences exist for being late. You've organized all that data into a model that helps you understand a complex real world system just enough to answer a question or make a decision.

"When should I leave home?"

Most complex systems rely on emergent behavior — high-level effects that arise indirectly from low-level interactions. Think about how traffic emerges from the interactions of everyone doing their best to answer the question "when should I leave home?"

Travel is a complex system for which we have a huge amount of experiential data. What about systems that are unfamiliar to us, systems where we don't have much experience or data? How do we explore those complex systems?

When exploring emergent systems the challenge lies in navigating a path towards progressively understanding more about it without getting lost. As we explore and discover we develop an intuition to guide our understanding.

Our intuition is typically built around creating new models of the system we're exploring. Modeling is thus a process of exploration and discovery.

How do we explore? If you move to a new city, you might learn the territory by walking around. Or you might peruse a map. But far more effective than either is both together — a street-level experience with higher-level guidance.

Likewise, the most powerful way to gain insight into a system is by moving between levels of abstraction. In real life, we do this instinctively. We know when we're late to school. We can stop and think about how many times we've been late this week. We try to find a patterns in what seems to be making us late. Or patterns in how to avoid the consequences of being late. We often do this implicity. And sometimes we get stuck. It's easy to get stuck on the ground, experiencing concrete day-to-day events with no higher-level view. "Ugh. I'm late again! I'm late every day!" It's also easy to get stuck in the clouds, working entirely with abstract ideas, and lose track of the ground level details. "I'm late, but in the larger scheme of things, maybe I'm right on time."

When we're working on problems further away from our direct every-day experience, we need to make our exploration explicit.

This interactive essay implements two techniques for making our modeling and our thinking more explicit.

The first is called the ladder of abstraction, and specifically, it's a technique for thinking explicitly about these levels, so a thinker can move among abstractions consciously and confidently.

The second is called iterative modeling, and specifically, it's a technique for thinking explicitly about using past experience to build new models to explore new questions, so a thinker can move among many different related models consciously and confidently.

I share the belief that an essential skill of the modern citizen will be using the interactive medium to move fluidly around the ladder of abstraction, and thoughtfully between models, so that they can more rapidly build a deeper and more thoughtful understanding of complex systems.

Let's Play a Game

The narrative above is a perfect starting place. Underneath that narrative is a model, where we start with a certain number of eggs, and then lose them as events in the spawning life cycle occur. This is called an event-based model. And now that you've gone through the model in text and interactive numbers, it's time for students to go through the model in an event-driven classroom game called the salmon game.

The game covers the journey of 2,600 salmon eggs as they leave their mothers pouch, and try to become an adult salmon that eventually returns to lay eggs of their own. The journey of the eggs consists of 17 different events, traversing 4 different life stages, and 6 different habitats.

At each event, either some number of potential offspring are lost, or the surving offspring move into a new habitat, a new life stage, or both.

We'll keep track of these 2,600 eggs and the events and life stages through which they progress using the numbers and events described to the right.

Students will keep track of the 2,600 eggs using beans, and you'll guide them through the events. A 1lb. bag of pinto beans contains approximately 1300 beans. Two pounds = 2600 beans, the approximate average number of eggs laid by 1 female coho.

100 beans have a mass of approxmiately 34 grams. When removing beans that are not in groups of 100, have students remove the nearest 100 based on mass, and then count out the beans needed to reach the total.

At each event either:
Remove individuals from population
Progress individuals to next developmental stage
Move individuals to their next habitat location
Abstracting

The game detailed above is actually a model of coho salmon spawning, supported by empirical data. It's a specific type of model called an event based model where time doesn't progress in days or months, but instead time progresses in a series of events.

In the game, this series of events becomes natural to think about, because the events in the game are concrete, descriptive and fit a narrative. This is a great place to start modeling, because it builds a natural comfort and intuition with the system.

We're going to rely on that foundation to begin modeling more abstractly. We do this for two reasons. The first is that if we want to continue to explore the reproductive strategies of the coho salmon, and we limit ourselves to counting beans every time, we won't have enough time to explore very much.

The second reason for progressively building abstraction into this concrete model is that abstraction is powerful. If we can thoughtfully abstract across complexity, we can make complexity easier to manage, and refocus our thinking on new questions and new problems.

Computational modeling allows us to organize and think more abstractly, so that we can empower ourselves to explore complex systems more deeply and thoughtfully, and use the data that we generate to support hypothesis and explanations and to create more robust arguments based on data.

How can we design systems when we don't know what we're doing?

The most exciting engineering challenges lie on the boundary of theory and the unknown. Not so unknown that they're hopeless, but not enough theory to predict the results of our decisions. Systems at this boundary often rely on emergent behavior — high-level effects that arise indirectly from low-level interactions.

When designing at this boundary, the challenge lies not in constructing the system, but in understanding it. In the absence of theory, we must develop an intuition to guide our decisions. The design process is thus one of exploration and discovery.

How do we explore? If you move to a new city, you might learn the territory by walking around. Or you might peruse a map. But far more effective than either is both together — a street-level experience with higher-level guidance.

Likewise, the most powerful way to gain insight into a system is by moving between levels of abstraction. Many designers do this instinctively. But it's easy to get stuck on the ground, experiencing concrete systems with no higher-level view. It's also easy to get stuck in the clouds, working entirely with abstract equations or aggregate statistics.

This interactive essay presents the ladder of abstraction, a technique for thinking explicitly about these levels, so a designer can move among them consciously and confidently.
